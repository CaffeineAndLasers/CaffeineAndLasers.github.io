My Book Library

Welcome to the library! This is something along the lines of my personal "Goodreads". I am a chronic people pleaser so a standerd out of 10 rating will never work for me.

Instead I have a very simple rating system,

  • üü• - I actively disliked it. You wont find many of these, since if I don't like a book I will just stop reading it, and even if I do finish reading it, I probably don't feel like writing about it.
  • üüß - Its decent. I finished it, I probably enjoyed it but I wont go so far as to recommend it.
  • üü© - I recommend It. I enjoyed it, and I would recommend it to others.
  • ‚≠ê - It changed me. This one is reserved for books that I can point to exactly how they changed me. These are the books that I will be thinking about for years to come.

Being Human

Author: Lewis Dartnell

Read: January 2026

Rating: üüß

Non-Fiction

The book promises a lot, the concept is an investigation to how biology has shaped human society and history. For a while it manages to hold up to that promise, the first few chapters are genuinely really interesting. By the midpoint of the book it starts to drag unfortunately, while the first few chapters investigate where cooperation and altruism comes from, the middle starts to talk about how disease affected colonialism, and how cognitive biases lead to poor decisions in leaders. Important? Yes. Groundbreaking? Not exactly.

Careless People

Author: Sarah Wynn-Williams

Read: December 2025

Rating: üü©

Non-Fiction

I knew Facebook did shady shit beforehand but the degree to which and the amount they knew that they were doing still managed to shock me. the metaphor the title implies, a reference to the Great Gatsby is extremely apt. The belligerence with which the Facebook leadership acted kind of blew me away. On the other hand, it is important to remember that this is and ex employee with an extra grind and she is bringing out all the dirty laundry. Personally, I tend to take a structural analysis point of view when talking about the problems with companies like Facebook. That Cory doctorow point about how the current cup of execs are not uniquely stupid or evil. They are just enabled by stupid laws. The book doesn't explore any of this, as far as Williams is concerned, the leadership at Facebook is uniquely evil and uncaring.

I remember when I had to study Great Gatsby in high school, we had to discuss the degree to which our narrator was a reliable narrator. Because he was enjoying all the same spoils as the people he was criticizing. I wonder about Williams. If she wasn't sexually harassed and bullied out of her job, I wonder how critical she would be of Facebook. Would she have still been a whistleblower if she wasn't treated so horribly by the people in charge? I don't know, maybe.

More Everything Forever

Author: Adam Becker

Read: November 2025

Rating: üüß

Non-Fiction

This ends up feeling like one of those documentaries about flat Earth consspiracy theorests, except the nutjobs in question are Peter Theil, Sam Altman and Venture Captialists. I found it frustrating at some points because of the ammount of time it spends debunking obviously stupid ideas. It definately brought up some insights into ideology of some of these people, but the book felt a little bit too long IMHO. The ideology it describes is so incoherent that I find myself wondering how useful understanding it really is. If the actions of longtermist effective altruists are indistinguishable from a selfish asshole, then I wonder why not just apply Occam's Razor and assume they are just selfish assholes?

Rating: üü©*

Non-Fiction

*If you willingly read the finance stories in the news or know a bit about personal finance, you can probably skip this book. But I would highly recommend buying for any teenagers or twenty-somethings in your life. (I know I would have liked to have read it earlier in my life.)

Author: Adam Higginbotham

Read: November 2025

Rating: üü©

Non-Fiction

It is embarrassing for me to admit that I work in the "Space Industry" but I barely know anything about space history, so I picked up this one.It was one of the most compelling non-fiction books I have ever read. Life As No One Knows It

Author: Sara Imari Walker

Read: September 2025

Rating: ‚≠ê

Non-Fiction

This was a great read as a physicist. The book was written with quite a high level of assumed knowledge, so if thermodynamics sounds like a scary word, I might suggest reading something more accessible. For me though, the premise was very interesting and prompted me to go and read a number of the papers cited in the book. From what I can tell, the central concept of "Assembly Theory" is somewhat controversial in Astrobiology circles, so I am not sure I am fully on board with the idea, but it is very interesting to engage with and think about. The book has me thinking a lot about the distinction between systems with selection, random systems and designed systems. I may have to write another blogpost about these ideas. Maybe one about Assembly Theory as well.

Algospeak

Author: Adam Aleksic

Read: September 2025

Rating: üüß

Non-Fiction

I was maybe a little bit more educated than the target audience for the book, because I found a lot of the points made a little bit obvious, and I was waiting for new information. The book is well written however, and has a very nice flow to it. I listened to it as an audiobook, so even got to listen to Aleksik perform the accents he was describing. Aleksik seems to be more optimistic about our algorithm future than I am, and I am not sure he convinced me. (I may just be cranky and cynical however). The one point I can confidently state that I disagree with: I think Aleksik overstates the effect of accents, because I think he over-estimates the proportion of posters-to-consumers on social media. I cannot see how a micro-cultural accent can occur when the line of communication is mostly mono-directional.

Everything Is Tuberculosis

Author: John Green

Read: August 2025

Rating: üü©

Non-Fiction
